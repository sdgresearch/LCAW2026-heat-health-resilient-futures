Green Walls of Privilege: How Tree Cover Tracks Deprivation Across England

An airborne, building-level assessment of the 3-30-300 greening rule across England — and who it leaves behind.

Every resident should see at least 3 trees from home, live in a neighbourhood with 30% canopy cover, and be within 300 metres of a public park.

The 3-30-300 rule — adopted from Singapore to New York, yet never tested at national scale

The same country looks green or grey — depending on the metric

Cities still lack standardised, high-resolution ways to measure who actually benefits from green infrastructure. The stakes are concrete: view England's trees per person and the rural North looks greenest; switch to trees per km² and the dense, affluent South East pulls ahead, with many areas exceeding 3,000 trees per km². The metric you pick changes where greening looks most needed.

Per capita and absolute density invert the map — a contradiction that coarse, inconsistent indicators routinely hide.

One framework, national scale

We built the first open-source, national, building-level assessment of the 3-30-300 rule — pairing open LiDAR with high-performance computing (Apache Sedona and Google Earth Engine) to measure tree proximity, canopy availability and park accessibility for every building in England.

Trees yes, canopy and parks no

Only 0.1% of urban neighbourhoods satisfy the full 3-30-300 rule. The '3' is the easy win — in London almost 40% of 8.2 million residents live within 25 m of at least three trees. But the 30% canopy target is the great national deficit, met by only a minority of people in every region, and park access trails the tree rule. Put all three together and the system nearly collapses.

A green gap that tracks wealth

Break each metric down by deprivation decile and a systematic pattern appears. In every region, from the North West to London, the least-deprived neighbourhoods have more trees nearby and higher canopy cover than the most-deprived. Ambient greenness — the street trees and garden canopy that surround a home — is consistently skewed toward wealthier communities.

Canopy cover and tree proximity rise with affluence in every single region of England.

Parks flip the script — and leafy regions hide the sharpest divides

Park access runs the other way: the most-deprived, densest urban cores are often closer to a public park than affluent suburbs, a reverse green gap. Yet the greenest places conceal the worst inequality — a Gini analysis flags Inner London, Birmingham and Manchester as hotspots of unequal access to both nearby trees and parks.

Green access is a new axis of inequality

Treating proximity, canopy and park access as distinct dimensions reveals exposure profiles that a single greenness index like NDVI obscures — and that vary systematically with deprivation and urban form. Distance to a park says nothing about its quality, safety or usability; a canopy percentage ignores what those trees actually do. The real power of the 3-30-300 rule is diagnostic.

Policy must measure not just how much nature a city has, but who can actually use it.
