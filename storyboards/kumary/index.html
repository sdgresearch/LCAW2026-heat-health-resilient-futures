Who Bears the Heat?

Mapping unequal urban heat exposure across Nairobi's wards
and the 1948 planning legacy still written into the city's thermal landscape.

The Study Area

Nairobi is the capital city of Kenya, a vital commercial and economic hub for East Africa. With a population of 5.8 million and annual population growthrate of 4.07% as of 2026, the city is a primary strategic gateway to East and Central Africa. Drag to pan, use the controls to zoom.

A City Doubling in Size

Over the last five decades Nairobi's built-up area has more than doubled from roughly 48 km² in 1975 to 103.35 km² in 2025. Much of that growth has been unregulated, producing extensive informal urbanization.

By 2050, the city is projected to be home to 14 million people.

…and Getting Hotter Every Year

Four decades of climate records show Nairobi warming at +0.0422 °C per year (95% CI: +0.0344 to +0.0500) — a total of +1.82 °C over the record, with heatwaves now more frequent and intense.

But that heat is not shared equally. The question is: who bears it?

Mapping the Heat Island

Using open geospatial datasets, we mapped satellite-derived urban heat island intensity across Nairobi's built-up area and classified it into five intensity classes.

Overlaying gridded population data lets us count exactly how many people live under each level of heat ward by ward.

The Burden in Numbers

Where Density Meets Heat

This bivariate map shows where high population density coincides with high heat exposure the darker the red-brown, the more people packed into the hottest parts of the city.

The pattern is unmistakable: extreme heat concentrates in informal settlements, where the urban poor live.

Key Insight: The most exposed wards —: Dandora Area Three (over 70% of residents exposed), Viwandani, Kariobangi South, Kwa Njenga — are dense, low-income neighbourhoods in the city's east.

A Pattern Drawn in 1948

Heat exposure clusters are statistically significant: hotspots (red) crowd the industrial east while coldspots (blue) trace the leafy, low-density west.

Set this map beside Thornton White's 1948 "masterplan for a colonial capital" and the divide aligns. Nairobi's legacy of segregated planning strongly correlates with present-day heat distribution — and a Lorenz curve confirms a very small fraction of the population bears a massively disproportionate share of the heat burden.

Heat Is an Equity Issue

In Nairobi, urban heat is not merely a physical phenomenon but a profound social one; inequality is quite literally printed in different hues across the city's thermal landscape. Beyond the usual suspects of lost greenery, building density and reduced sky-view factors, historical planning injustice shapes who swelters today.

Addressing this growing public-health threat starts with accurately identifying the most vulnerable communities. Nairobi's informal settlements should be the priority targets for heat intervention, before climate change and rapid urbanization push exposure even higher.
